## Introduction to Money
Money is a system of value that facilitates the exchange of goods and services in an economy. Before money, people used a barter system, which was inefficient and required a "double coincidence of wants"—meaning you had to find someone who had what you wanted and also wanted what you had.

### The Three Primary Functions of Money
Money serves three crucial roles in any modern economy:

1.  **Medium of Exchange**
    Money acts as an intermediary instrument to avoid the inefficiencies of barter. It is widely accepted in payment for goods, services, and debts.

2.  **Store of Value**
    Money allows you to save your purchasing power from the time you earn it until the time you spend it. While inflation can erode this value over time, money is generally more stable than holding perishable goods.

3.  **Unit of Account**
    Money provides a common standard for measuring the market value of goods, services, and transactions. It allows us to easily compare the cost of completely different items.

## Key Concepts Explained

### The Problem with Barter
Bartering is the direct exchange of goods and services without a medium of money. Its main problems are:
- **Lack of a Common Measure:** How many bananas is one cow worth? Without a standard unit, valuing goods is difficult.
- **Indivisibility:** You cannot easily divide a large item (like a cow) to pay for a smaller one (like a basket of fruit).
- **The "Double Coincidence of Wants" Problem:** You must find a trading partner who has what you want *and* wants what you have, which is highly inefficient.

### Why Does Money Have Value?
- **Commodity Money:** Historically, money derived its value from the physical substance it was made of, such as gold or silver.
- **Fiat Money:** Modern money (like the Rupee or Dollar) is **fiat currency**. It has value because a government declares it as legal tender and because people have faith in its stability and widespread acceptance. It is not backed by a physical commodity.

## Real-World Example
Imagine you are a tailor. You stitch a shirt and sell it for ₹800. That ₹800 is a token of your work. You can now use it to buy your child's books (₹500) and vegetables for the week (₹300). You didn't need to find a bookseller who wanted a shirt or a grocer who needed your tailoring skills.

## Common Questions

**Q: Is digital money in my bank account real money?**
A: Yes. The balance in your bank account or digital wallet represents real value and is a modern form of money. It is often called digital currency or bank money.

**Q: Why can't the government just print more money for everyone?**
A: Printing money without a corresponding increase in goods and services in the economy leads to **inflation**. If everyone has more money but the amount of products to buy stays the same, prices will simply rise, reducing the purchasing power of each unit of currency.
